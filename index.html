DISCUSSION: 

Cerro de Pasco, a city in central Peru, has a long history of mining activities, particularly for lead, copper, zinc, and silver.36 About 10% of GDP comes from mining, and in 2022, mining exports were worth $66 billion, or roughly 57% of all exports by value. Since the beginning of the twentieth century, Cerro de Pasco has been mined for industrial purposes. However, its mining history dates back to the 17th century, when prospectors were drawn to the area by its lead, zinc, and silver wealth, making it Peru's second-largest city.37 

 

However, heavy metal concentrations in soil and water are far beyond national and World Health Organisation (WHO) safety regulations, resulting from these operations' substantial environmental contamination. For example, Peruvian standards allow up to 0.05 mg/L of lead in drinking water, although WHO guidelines call for 0.01 mg/L. This regulatory disparity puts the local population's health at risk.38 

But despite these alarming findings, the Peruvian government and Volcan, the mining company operating in Cerro de Pasco, have been criticised for inadequate remediation efforts and have done little to nothing to remediate contamination and respond to the communities’ pleas for help. 

 

According to a report39 According to local officials released last year, at least 2,000 children in the Pasco region suffer from chronic heavy metal toxicity as a result of the mining operations' inaction (a disorder that, particularly in children under six, can have a significant impact on both mental and physical development because heavy meats adhere to cells)40, Furthermore, the city's mining waste impacts the water supply for more than 100,000 people. According to investigations, the San Juan River, which runs from Cerro de Pasco south to Junín Lake, contains at least four heavy metals in concentrations higher than WHO guidelines (the WHO recommends 0.01 mg/L of lead in water, while Peru's is 0.05 mg/L), including arsenic and lead. 41 

 

In response to enquiries, the government stated that the water and air quality falls within the national norm set by the National Standard for the Environmental Quality of Air. Implying that Cerro de Pasco's air and water were uncontaminated by the mining. However, they refused to respond when it was brought up that the WHO's criteria for heavy metal concentrations in water are more stringent and that they would like to clarify their remarks accordingly. It calls into question how well the current environmental laws and their implementation safeguard the public's health.  

 
When the Natural Resource Governance Institute (a group devoted to enhancing nations' management of their natural resources42),  Peru received a "satisfactory" 62/100 rating from the non-profit Natural Resource Governance Institute (NRGI) last year, but Carlos Monge, NRGI's director for Latin America, is concerned that ambition may entice profitable mining projects to Peru, which could result in a deterioration of environmental standards and a further escalation of public health risks.43  

In conclusion, the Cerro de Pasco situation emphasises the urgent need for stronger environmental laws, better enforcement of current standards, and preventative actions to alleviate the serious negative effects of mining-related pollution on human health.
